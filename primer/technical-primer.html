Silo 1

Ixana & Bioelectric Communication

The cow as the wire.

Standard radio waves — Wi-Fi, Bluetooth — are easily absorbed by biological tissue. A cow is, in essence, a giant bag of water and meat. To solve connectivity, CacheCow uses Body Channel Communication (BCC): instead of blasting signals through the air, BCC uses capacitive coupling to turn the natural conductive properties of skin and tissue into the transmission medium.

1
The medium — skin as conductor
The sensor couples directly to the skin, using the animal's own biology as the wire. No RF interference, no signal absorption, no need for external antenna infrastructure in metal barns.
2
The data — the autonomic nervous system
Because the sensor is physically coupled to the skin, it listens directly to the Autonomic Nervous System. It captures heart rate variability and stress responses — the first indicators of a viral or bacterial infection — within 12–18 hours of onset, long before any behavioral change is visible.
3
The accuracy — 99.9% individual ID
The direct physical link eliminates signal interference from the rest of the herd. Each data stream is uniquely bound to a specific animal, making the identity layer as precise as the health layer.

Analogy — The Nervous System Polygraph

Think of BCC as a polygraph for the nervous system. While a cow might "lie" to a rancher by standing up and pretending to be healthy — prey-animal instinct — its autonomic nervous system cannot hide the physiological stress of an escalating infection. The body always tells the truth. BCC is how we listen to it.

The accidental breakthrough

The copper coil miracle.

Hardware engineering in agriculture faces a hard problem: metal barns and industrial feedlots block cellular and GPS signals — the Faraday Cage effect. The solution was discovered while solving a completely different problem.

1
The cooling need
Engineers added a copper coil around the edge of the ear tag as a passive heat sink — a standard approach to dissipating thermal energy from densely packed electronics.
2
The discovery
A loop of copper wire is, by definition, a magnetic loop antenna. The heat sink was simultaneously solving the connectivity problem — and providing unexpected EMP hardening for the internal circuitry.
3
The infrastructure hack
Most industrial barns contain derelict copper telephone lines (POTS — Plain Old Telephone Service). The tag's coil inductively couples with these wires. Data jumps from the ear tag to the old phone lines in the walls, turning legacy infrastructure into a passive network. The indoor connectivity problem for industrial barns is solved without a single Ethernet cable.
